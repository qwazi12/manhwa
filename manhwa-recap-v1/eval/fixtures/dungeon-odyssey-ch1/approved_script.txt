The war with the labyrinth had raged for decades — humanity against the
things that boiled up from below, soldiers emptying their guns into
biomechanical horrors while monsters charged in roaring hordes.

It ended in victory for the ones above ground. Soldiers planted their flag
on a mountain of monster carcasses against the sunset.

And when it was over, thousands of prisoners of that war walked up out of
the dark toward the light.

Among them were children born inside the labyrinth itself — infants who had
never once seen the sky.

Their mothers were already dead. Something else had raised them down there,
cradled in skeletal hands beneath a blood-red moon, weaned on monsters'
milk.

Years later, guards at the cave mouth watched one such survivor emerge — a
small boy wrapped in a blanket, blue sparks crawling over his skin. It's
safe now, child, they told him, and warned each other to be careful,
because he clearly couldn't control his powers yet.

The world called children like him dungeon babies. This one grew into a
scarred, somber young man the media never tired of pointing cameras at.

Which is exactly why a film crew had hired him. Their bright-eyed producer
had done her homework, leaning in eagerly to share it: everyone knew
dungeon babies were obsessed with dungeons.

Except this one. He was famous as the only dungeon baby who refused to even
go near the labyrinth — and the only one whose level had never been
revealed. Maybe he was just weak, the crew joked. She had hired guards,
just in case.

He led them down anyway, repeating his rules: keep moving, avoid conflict,
and no guns. They laughed him off — dungeon baby or Buddha baby, just guide
us properly. He swallowed his frustration, thinking that while they wasted
time on small talk, someone in the labyrinth was being ripped to pieces.

Then something found them. The guards opened fire — exactly what he had
told them not to do — and a massive reptilian monster's roar drowned out
the gunshots as grown men clutched their ears.

When the dust settled, the workers stood frozen — then burst into shaky,
sweat-soaked laughter and insisted it was nothing.

The hunters crowed over the carcass. All moving things die when shot, they
declared; the monsters down here were just animals after all.

He told them to throw out what they didn't need and turn back. The biggest
of them clenched a fist over the nervous porter and scoffed — what was he
talking about? The situation was handled.

The mountain answered. A dull, heavy pounding rolled through the cavern,
and cracks crawled through the stone.

What's that sound, someone whispered. A predator heard the gunshots, he
said flatly. It was still far away — if they ran for the exit right now,
they could make it. The hunters had a better idea: let's fight.

They huddled and planned. Lure it onto a tight path and shoot it down — the
last one dropped after a few shots, and its corpse was worth millions. Two
kills each and they'd clear ten million apiece. This had been their goal
all along.

He didn't argue with greed. Do as you wish, he said, turning his back —
we'll stay away, since we don't have weapons. What you kill is your pay, so
don't come asking for a share later.

Then he looked back with a glow rising in his eye and ordered the rest to
follow him, and hurry — cutting off the one who whined about filming. The
hunters waved them off cheerfully, promising to see them later.

He set a brutal pace down the rock slopes until the whole crew was gasping.
No more filming, he said. We're leaving.

The producer started to protest — then stopped mid-sentence, wide-eyed, as
it finally clicked: the reason the only dungeon baby in the world refused
to go near the labyrinth was never weakness.

Gunfire echoed through the dark. The group froze under a shower of falling
dust, counting shot after shot after shot — until the shots stopped.

The nearby predators heard those gunshots, he told her — and the ones who
killed first had marked themselves as prey. She demanded to know if he had
let them die knowing that. If he had tried to convince them, he said, they
would all be dead — the blood scent soaked the guards' clothes, and anyone
running beside them would have been caught too. Then even the distant
sounds stopped, and so did their voices.

Somewhere behind them, an abandoned camera kept recording as an armored
fist punched into a screaming, fanged mouth.

The things that had come were wolf monkeys — white-furred apes with glowing
red eyes. One chewed a man alive. Another left a helmeted boy coughing
black blood.

Wolf monkeys?! someone screamed. Why were they on the first floor?

A whole pack had already caught up — as if they had known the group's
location from the very start. One snatched a man in a hard hat clean off
the ground.

And one of them — tusked, twice a man's height — roared directly behind
him. A blue spark cracked across his eye.

He stopped hiding. In a heartbeat he was on the beast's back, a glowing
dagger in his gloved hand, and he drove it into the monster's neck as it
howled.

He leapt and slashed down through another, while below, a second monster
raised its fists over the terrified camera girl.

He snatched her up under one arm. I'm going to run at the pace you'd expect
of a dungeon baby, he said.

A few ribs might break — just deal with it. He leapt clear with two
passengers on his back as the ape crashed into the rock behind them,
roaring.

Safe and shaking, the girl turned on him through streaming tears: he was
strong enough to fight with a dull knife — so why did he let the others
die?

His answer was ice. He was hired as a guide, not a guard — nothing in the
contract said fight. That's Gyser for you. She was the one who had hired a
pack of arrogant idiots who had never been down here, and they had done
everything he told them not to do. So what did she mean, why didn't he save
them?

Then he held out his hand. He was still working, and she still needed to
pay. I'll keep you alive.

The embers began to drift, and he froze where he stood.

Torchlight. A stone relief of a green serpent coiled around a sword. The
labyrinth emblem.

The surviving boy admitted he had just run in any direction — and they had
ended up inside the labyrinth itself. They needed to get out, right now.
The stone answered with a crack.

The ground split under their feet and collapsed, and they could only stare
as the abyss swallowed everything downward.

He came to flat on his back, staring up into the dark — then bolted
upright.

His left arm was broken. He looked at it, and allowed that it could have
been worse.

It got worse. A tail coiled around him and squeezed — a colossal
insect-headed thing with glowing blue eyes — and it was fast, its fanged
jaws already lunging for him.

Broken-armed, bound in the dark, at the bottom of the labyrinth he had
spent his whole life refusing to enter — his story was only beginning.
