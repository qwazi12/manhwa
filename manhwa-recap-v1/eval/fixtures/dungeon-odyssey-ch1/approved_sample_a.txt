SCRIPT A — ENRICHED
Dungeon Odyssey, Chapter 1 (30-panel sample)
Narrator may infer motives, backstory, and emotional subtext the panels imply.

Long before this story began, humanity had already learned to plant flags on
mountains of monster bones. Soldiers stood over the skeletal remains of the
things they had killed and raised their banner against the sunset, and whole
crowds walked up out of the dark caves toward the light, certain the worst was
behind them.

It wasn't behind everyone. When guards at a cave mouth shouted that one more
was coming, what emerged from that darkness was not a soldier but a child — a
small, dark-haired boy clutching a tattered blanket, his eyes burning an
unnatural electric blue. Someone laid a reassuring hand on his shoulder and
told him he was safe now, while another voice warned to be careful, because
the boy clearly could not control his powers yet. He was one of the dungeon's
own — born of it, marked by it, and feared for it from the very first day.

Years later, that boy had grown into a quiet young man in a long coat, hiring
himself out as a guide to expeditions descending into the labyrinth beneath
Gyser. He knew the place the way the others never could, and it showed in
every order he gave. He told the group it was too dangerous to rest until
they at least reached the second floor's entrance, and he repeated — again —
that the guards must not fire their guns no matter what they ran into. He
could see they weren't really listening, and it grated on him; while they
wasted time on small talk, he knew somewhere deeper in the labyrinth someone
was being torn to pieces. Gyser was not some sketchy neighborhood you could
swagger through.

The lesson arrived on schedule. A massive wolf-like beast bore down on the
miners, its roar so loud the men clutched their ears in agony — and the
hunters shot it dead anyway, exactly as he had told them not to. Standing
over the carcass they congratulated themselves, crowing that everything that
moves dies when you shoot it, that the monsters down here were just animals
after all. Their confidence was the confidence of men who had never been
down here before. He told them to dump whatever they didn't need and turn
back immediately. The biggest of the hunters loomed over the nervous porter
beside him and scoffed that the situation was already handled.

He didn't argue. He simply ordered the rest to follow him and hurry, cutting
off even the one who whined about needing to film. The brown-haired girl who
had hired on with the expedition was starting to piece him together — she
gasped as she realized why a dungeon baby like him refused to go near the
labyrinth he was born to.

Then the gunshots came, echoing through the dark, and he knew the group had
just been split in two. He kept his voice low so the others wouldn't panic,
but he understood exactly what the sound meant: every predator nearby had
heard it, and the ones who fired first had marked themselves as prey.

The apes found them fast. Red-eyed, white-furred, tusked — they snatched a
screaming miner in their grip while a monster twice a man's height roared at
the young guide's back. The stone gave way beneath him and he fell toward a
snarling beast waiting below, and in that moment he finally stopped hiding
what he was. His eyes lit violet. A glowing dagger carved through the giant
ape mid-roar; he swung his blade in wide, fluid arcs, leaping through the
dark, and finished the last beast with a downward slash and cold fury in his
glowing eyes.

He hauled the girl onto his back and ran, a gaping-jawed monster closing
behind them, telling her through gritted teeth that a few ribs might break
and she would just have to deal with it. He dove clear of a horned monster
crashing through the stone. When the woman who had organized the expedition
knelt before him afterward, he gave her no comfort: she was the one who had
hired a pack of arrogant idiots who had never been down here, they had done
everything he told them not to, and she had no right to ask why he hadn't
saved them.

The labyrinth had the last word. The floor split open into a vast collapsing
crater, and he and the girl could only stare as the abyss swallowed
everything downward. He landed alone in a ruined chamber at what he grimly
recognized as the center of the labyrinth — the one place no one returns
from. Above a stone altar loomed a huge carving of a serpent coiled around a
sword, and before he could even take it in, something answered his arrival:
a colossal insectoid thing with glowing blue eyes wrapped its tail around
him and began to squeeze.

His story as prey — or as something far worse than the monsters — was only
beginning.
